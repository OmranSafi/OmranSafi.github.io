About Me


I am an academic motivated to furthering human knowledge and accomplishment. I am currently pursuing a Bachelor of Arts degree in Psychology at the University of British Columbia. I enjoy exploring a number of topics in an interdisciplinary and diverse environment. I believe that diversity of thought and diversity of representation in unison with interdisciplinary work is a formula for success in any field. Some topics that interest me are Consciousness, Memory, Perception, Attention, AI, AI Ethics, and Climate Change. While my main area of interest is Psychology/Neuroscience, I also love reading and learning about evolution, astrophysics, astrobiology, and ecology.

Although I have spent the majority of my life in the beautiful city of Vancouver, I consider myself a citizen of the world. I love learning about and learning from different peoples and cultures of the world. Growing up in Vancouver ingrained an appreciation and love of nature in me that has persisted through the years. When I am not pursuing my academic goals, I enjoy spending time outdoors and can often be found hiking, rock-climbing, snowboarding or mountaineering.

My Research



I am currently involved in research with the Motivated Cognition Lab and Behavioral Sustainability Lab in the Department of Psychology and the Behavioral Reward Affect & Impulsivity Neuroscience (BRAIN) lab in the Department of Psychiatry at the University of British Columbia.

My current research, being run across the Behavioral Sustainability and Motivated Cognition labs, is examining the consequences of affect generated from the overview effect on climate change attitudes and behaviors. The study employs virtual reality technology and a virtual rendering of Earth made in Unity to expose participants to the overview effect while measuring physiological and behavioral responses.

In addition to my personal research, I am also working with the UBC BRAIN lab in a research assistant capacity. My current work at the BRAIN lab focuses on using a portable EEG headband called the Muse to assess implicit craving in clinical populations with concurrent stimulant use and mental disorders. I am also working on a project that aims to use this technology to find a novel biomarker for craving and relapse.
